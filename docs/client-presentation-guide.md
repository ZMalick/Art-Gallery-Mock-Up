# Client Website Presentation Guide
## For Kay's Originals — Presenting to Kay Green

---

## Do You Need a Slide Deck?

**Short answer: Not really, but a light one helps.**

The industry standard for presenting a finished website to a client is a **live demo as the main event**, with a few slides as a wrapper to open and close. The website itself is the deliverable — slides just provide structure and context.

| Approach | When It Works | Risk |
|----------|--------------|------|
| **Slides only** (screenshots pasted into a deck) | Never recommended | Kay sees static images instead of the real, functioning site |
| **Live demo only** (no slides at all) | Small informal meeting, strong existing relationship | Can feel unstructured; no clear opening or closing |
| **Hybrid** (2-3 opening slides, live demo, 1-2 closing slides) | Best for most situations | None — this is the recommended approach |

**For your team:** Use the hybrid approach. Open with 2-3 context slides (3-5 minutes), do the live walkthrough (15-20 minutes), close with 1-2 slides on next steps and open feedback. Total: 25-30 minutes.

---

## Slide Deck Structure (5-7 Slides)

Keep slides minimal — they frame the demo, they don't replace it.

| Slide | Content | Time |
|-------|---------|------|
| **1. Title** | "Kay's Originals Website" — team members, date, Kay's name | 30 sec |
| **2. Goals Recap** | "Here's what you asked for" — bullet the original requirements Kay gave you (informational gallery, showcase artists/artwork, consignment info, clean and professional) | 1-2 min |
| **3. Design Approach** | 2-3 bullet points: editorial aesthetic inspired by gallery sites, colors derived from Kay's logo, mobile-responsive. Include one homepage screenshot. | 1-2 min |
| **— LIVE DEMO —** | Switch to the browser — this is the core of the presentation | 15-20 min |
| **4. Features Summary** | Quick bullet recap: gallery with filters/search, artist profiles, consignment page, FAQ, contact, mobile responsive | 1 min |
| **5. Next Steps** | What happens now — feedback, revisions, hosting, handoff | 1-2 min |
| **6. Questions** | "What stands out to you? Does this align with what you envisioned?" | Open |

**Slide rules:**
- Bullet points, not paragraphs — Kay is looking at slides, not reading essays
- One or two real screenshots from the site (homepage, gallery page)
- Do NOT put every page on a slide — that is what the live demo is for
- Do NOT lead with team bios or a long intro about yourselves

## Live Demo Walkthrough

The walkthrough is the heart of the presentation. Structure it as a **user journey**, not a page-by-page feature tour. Walk Kay through the site the way a customer or artist would experience it.

### Recommended Flow

**1. Homepage (2-3 min)**
Show the hero slideshow, navigation, and overall feel. Point out the logo integration, the color scheme, and how the page sets the tone for the gallery.

> "When someone finds your gallery online, this is the first thing they see — the rotating artwork immediately tells them this is an art gallery, and the navigation lets them explore from here."

**2. Gallery Page (3-4 min)**
Browse artwork, use the category filters, try the search, click into an artwork detail page. Frame it as the customer experience.

> "A visitor can browse all artwork, filter by paintings, sculptures, or sketches, and search for something specific. When they find a piece they like, they click in and see the full details."

**3. Artwork Detail (1-2 min)**
Show the large image, description, specs, and the link back to the artist. Emphasize that every artwork connects to its creator.

> "Every piece links back to the artist who made it — so if a customer loves a painting, they can immediately see who the artist is and explore more of their work."

**4. Artists Page (2-3 min)**
Show the artist grid, use the search, click into one artist profile. Show how artwork is linked to the artist.

> "This is where visitors learn about the talented artists who show at your gallery. Each artist has a full profile with their bio and all their work in one place."

**5. Consignment Page (2 min)**
Show the information and application process for artists looking to consign work. This is the artist-facing audience path.

> "This page speaks to artists who are interested in showing their work at Kay's Originals. It explains the process and gives them a way to reach out."

**6. About, Contact, FAQ (2-3 min)**
Quick walkthrough of supporting pages. Don't linger unless Kay has questions.

> "These pages round out the site — visitors can learn about the gallery's story, get in touch, or find answers to common questions."

**7. Mobile Responsiveness (2 min)**
Resize the browser window or pull up the site on a phone. This is always a crowd-pleaser — clients are often surprised and delighted when their site works well on mobile.

> "Everything we just showed you works on phones and tablets too. If you're in the gallery and want to show someone the site, it looks great on any device."

### Demo Tips
- **Move deliberately** — don't rush through pages
- **Narrate as a story** — "Imagine a customer finds your gallery online..."
- **Pause for questions** during the demo, not just at the end
- **Have the site pre-loaded** with no other browser tabs open
- **Keep the browser zoomed in** enough that everyone in the room can see
- **Have a backup plan** — a screen recording or screenshots saved locally in case the internet fails

## What to Say (and What NOT to Say)

### Framing Design Decisions

Every design choice should be explained in terms of **Kay's goals and her audience**, not technical details.

| Instead of... | Say... |
|---------------|--------|
| "We used position: fixed with a z-index on the nav" | "The navigation stays visible as you scroll so visitors can always find their way around" |
| "We picked blue because we liked it" | "The blue comes directly from your logo — it creates a consistent brand identity across the site" |
| "We used CSS custom properties for the color system" | "All the colors are based on your logo's palette — blue, coral, teal, and gold" |
| "We built it in vanilla JS with no frameworks" | "The site is lightweight and fast — no heavy software to maintain" |
| "We thought it would be cool to add gold frames" | "The gold frame accents give the artwork cards a gallery feel — like each piece is framed and ready to hang" |

### Questions to Ask Kay

Do NOT ask "Do you like it?" — this invites vague subjective feedback. Instead:

- "Does this align with what you envisioned for your gallery's online presence?"
- "Does this work for the customers who visit your gallery?"
- "Is there anything that doesn't feel like you or your gallery?"
- "What stands out to you — positively or otherwise?"

### Things to Avoid
- **Don't apologize.** Never say "Sorry this isn't perfect" or "We ran out of time on this part." Present with confidence.
- **Don't use jargon.** Kay doesn't need to hear about CSS variables, IIFE modules, or DOM manipulation.
- **Don't get defensive.** When Kay suggests changes, write it down. Don't explain why your choice was "better."
- **Don't fill silences.** After you ask for feedback, be quiet and let Kay respond. Give her time to think.

## Design Decisions to Be Ready to Explain

These are the decisions Kay is most likely to notice or ask about:

| Decision | Talking Point |
|----------|--------------|
| Color palette | "Every color comes from your logo — the blue, coral, teal, gold, and charcoal. The site feels like an extension of the brand you already have." |
| Typography | "We paired an elegant serif font for headings with a clean modern font for body text — it feels editorial, like an art magazine." |
| Gold frame accents | "The gold borders on artwork cards give each piece a framed, gallery-ready feel." |
| Two-audience structure | "The site naturally splits into two paths — customers browse art and artists, while artists interested in consigning find their own dedicated page." |
| No e-commerce | "Per your direction, the site is purely informational — no prices, no shopping carts. The focus is on the art and the artists." |
| Terminology | "We used your terminology throughout — 'media' not 'mediums', 'sketches' not 'drawings'." |
| Vanilla HTML/CSS/JS | "The site has no heavy dependencies — it's lightweight, fast, and easy to maintain or hand off to anyone." |

## Questions Kay Will Likely Ask

| Question | Prepared Answer |
|----------|-----------------|
| "Can I update artwork or artist info myself?" | Yes — walk through how `data.js` works (show the file briefly) |
| "How do I add a new artist?" | Show the data format — name, bio, photo, media type |
| "Will customers be able to buy artwork?" | No — this is informational by design, per your request |
| "Where will this be hosted?" | Have options ready: GitHub Pages (free), Netlify (free), or her existing hosting provider |
| "What if something breaks?" | Discuss the maintenance plan — who to contact, what's covered |
| "Can I show this on my phone in the gallery?" | Yes — pull it up on a phone right there to demonstrate |
| "Does this represent my gallery?" | This is the real question. Listen carefully to her answer — it tells you everything. |
| "Can we change [specific element]?" | "Absolutely — let's write that down." Be open to feedback. |
| "What happens after today?" | Deployment, support period, revision rounds — have this planned |

## What Makes Clients Happiest

Research across multiple sources consistently identifies these as the top factors:

1. **"It looks like me."** The single biggest factor. Kay will light up if the site feels like her gallery — her logo prominent, her colors, her vibe. Clients care most about seeing their identity reflected back.

2. **Clean and easy to navigate.** Clients are not designers — they notice when things feel intuitive. Kay specifically values "clean and efficient."

3. **Mobile looks good.** Always demo this. It's consistently a crowd-pleaser.

4. **Their content is showcased.** Kay will care most about how the artwork and artists look. Make sure the gallery and artist pages look their best during the demo.

5. **Feeling heard.** Reference her original requirements during the demo: "You mentioned you wanted..." This builds trust and shows you listened.

6. **Professionalism of the presentation itself.** A structured, confident presentation signals competence. Kay will feel reassured that her site was built with care.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Sending a link instead of presenting live.** Never email the URL and say "take a look." Clients fixate on minor details without context. Always walk them through it.

2. **Jumping straight into the demo.** Always recap the goals first. Remind Kay what she asked for — then show how you delivered it.

3. **Showing everything.** A focused 20-minute walkthrough of the key paths beats a 45-minute feature tour. Less is more.

4. **Not rehearsing.** Practice the full walkthrough at least once as a team. Know who talks when. Unplanned presentations go off the rails.

5. **Technical difficulties.** Have the site loaded and tested before Kay arrives. Clear other browser tabs. Have a backup (screen recording or saved screenshots).

6. **Not defining next steps.** End every presentation with clear action items — what happens next, who does what, and when.

## Presentation Day Checklist

### Before Kay Arrives
- [ ] Test the site on Chrome, Firefox, and Safari
- [ ] Test on a phone or tablet (or browser dev tools)
- [ ] Have the slide deck open and ready
- [ ] Have the site pre-loaded in the browser — no other tabs
- [ ] Know the Wi-Fi password; have a mobile hotspot as backup
- [ ] Have a screen recording or screenshots saved locally as backup
- [ ] Print or have digital copies of the handoff doc ready
- [ ] Arrive 10-15 minutes early
- [ ] Assign roles — who presents which section, who takes notes on feedback

### During the Presentation
- [ ] Make eye contact with Kay, not the screen
- [ ] Narrate the demo as a user journey, not a feature list
- [ ] Pause for questions during the walkthrough
- [ ] Take written notes on every piece of feedback
- [ ] Ask targeted questions ("Does this align with your vision?") not vague ones ("Do you like it?")
- [ ] End with clear next steps

### Attire
Business casual or professional — match the tone of a gallery meeting.

## Handoff: What Kay Needs After the Presentation

### 1. All Site Files
Package the entire project directory (HTML, CSS, JS, images) in a ZIP. Since this is a static site with no build tools, the files are ready to deploy as-is.

### 2. Style Reference
A one-page summary listing:
- Fonts used (DM Serif Display, Outfit)
- Color hex codes (from the CSS variables)
- File structure overview (what file does what)

### 3. Hosting Guidance
A brief explanation of how to get the site live. Options for a static site:
- **GitHub Pages** — free, reliable
- **Netlify** — free tier, drag-and-drop deploy
- **Her existing hosting provider** — just upload the files

### 4. Content Update Instructions
A short guide explaining:
- How to add a new artist (editing `js/data.js`)
- How to add new artwork (editing `js/data.js`)
- Image naming and location conventions (`images/` folder)
- Note: requires basic familiarity with editing code files

### 5. Recorded Walkthrough (Optional but Impressive)
A short screen recording of the demo walkthrough that Kay can revisit later. This is a nice professional touch — clients forget details from meetings.

### 6. Clear Scope Communication
Be explicit about:
- Whether your team is available for future changes
- How many revision rounds are included
- What constitutes "maintenance" vs. "new work"
- Who to contact if something breaks
